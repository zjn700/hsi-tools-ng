getting Started

Welcome to the Human Systems Integration Risk Management Tool (HSI-RMT). This software suite consists of two sub-tools designed to guide Human Systems Integration (HSI) Analysts/Practitioners, Program Managers (PMs), and Chief Engineers, etc. through the process of accomplishing HSI best practices throughout system development. The first, the HSI Capabilities and Requirements Tool (HSI-CART) is designed to assist MAJCOM HSI Cells with the appropriate developmental planning and pre-Joint Capabilities Integration Development System (JCIDS) activities, such as Concept Development and Analysis of Alternatives. The second, the Program Risk Assessment Tool is designed to assist the system program office (SPO) with integrating HSI throughout the JCIDS process and acquisition life cycle, from Material Solution Analysis to Disposal planning. In this first section, we will introduce HSI and cover the basics of creating an account, logging in to your account, and basic navigation through the tool.

Introduction to Human Systems Integration

AFI 63-1201, Life Cycle Systems Engineering defines Human Systems Integration as "a disciplined, unified, and interactive systems engineering approach to integrate human considerations into the system development, design, and life cycle management to improve total system performance and reduce total costs of ownership." Those human considerations span nine U.S. Air Force recognized HSI domains:

Human Factors Engineering - Design characteristics for human capabilities and limitations

Manpower - Assessment of the human capital requirements (manning mix) necessary to operate, maintain, and support the system.

Personnel - Assessment of the attributes (knowledge, skills, and abilities) required to operate, maintain, and support the system.

Training - design and development of instruction, resources, and support required to impart system end users with the required knowledge, skills, and abilities necessary to operate, maintain, and support the system.

Environment - Assessment of those operational environmental factors and how they impact system design, as well as how the design impacts the operational environment.

Safety - Assessment of system design characteristics and associated procedures that serve to minimize mishap potential.

Occupational Health - Assessment of system design features that serve to minimize the risks for injury, illness, or disability, and enhance job performance.

Survivability - Consideration of system design features which reduce the likelihood for death or serious injury, to include consideration for hostile environments, fratricide, and emergency egress.

Habitability - Integrated considerations of the system and associated facilities’ impact social interactions, morale, quality of life, safety and comfort, etc. to sustain maximum performance.


Figure 1. Human Systems Integration

Human Systems Integration is a unique discipline in that is it both a technical and managerial philosophy. Per Department of Defense Instruction 5000.02, program managers are required to plan for and implement HSI through the product lifecycle. The earlier HSI is introduced, the more likely it is to reap the long-term benefits of risk mitigation, reduced redesign and rework, mishap reduction, etc. and achieve the desired life cycle cost savings. If properly included in system development, Analysts and Practitioners integrate and examine the risks and tradeoffs amongst the nine domains balance against life cycle cost considerations to provide consultation and decision making support to the Program Manager, ensuring that human considerations are adequately accounted for. In this manner, developers are able to drive total system performance and minimize total life cycle cost.

HSI is not just another name for Human Factors or just "good Systems Engineering". Rather, Human Factors is just one of the disciplines that Analysts integrates - HSI includes so much more. In fact, a primary role of the Analyst/Practitioner is to integrate each of their own domains as well as those from other functional leads to advise their Program Managers and Systems Engineers on the integrated tradeoffs.

Opening an Existing Project

There are two option for opening an existing project from the Project Landing Page (Figure 4). First, find the project you wish to work on. The first option is to click the "Tools" button, which will take you to the Tools page (discussed later in Section X.X). The

second option is to click the "Edit" button. Since the HSI-RMT incorporates a save-state function, this option will return you to the exact point where you left off the last time you worked on that project.

Finally, you are also able to delete any project by clicking the "Delete" button.

HSI Capabilities and Requirements Tool

As previously stated, the HSI-CART is designed to assist analysts working at the MAJCOM or other practitioners participating in early developmental activities, e.g. Concept Definition, Capabilities-Based Assessment, and Analysis of Alternatives. This tool is accessed by clicking the "CART" button on the Tool Selection page.


Figure 8. Capabilities and Requirements Tool Dashboard

The HSI-CART contains three questionnaires, one each for the primary early-development activities: Concept Development, Capabilities-Based Assessment, and Analysis of Alternatives. Each questionnaire has 10 sections, one each for the nine HSI domains as well as one general integration section. Each question is either answered "yes" or "no" and followed up with guided risk identification and management questions.

HSI Program Risk Analysis Tool

As previously stated, the HSI-PRAT is designed to assist analysts working at the SPO in performing HSI best practices through system lifecycle through the JCIDS process. This tool is accessed by clicking the "PRAT" button on the Tool Selection page.


Figure 9. Program Risk Analysis Tool Dashboard

The HSI-CART contains three questionnaires, one each for the primary JCIDS phases: Material Solutions Analysis, Technology Maturation and Risk Reduction, Engineering and Manufacturing Development, Production and Deployment, and Operations and Sustainment. Notice that the Disposal phase is not included in that list; this is because most HSI involvement with respect to this phase occurs during the Planning for Disposal activities accomplished earlier in the JCIDS process. Like the HSI-CART, each questionnaire has 10 sections, one each for the nine HSI domains as well as one general integration section. Each question is either answered "yes" or "no" and followed up with guided risk identification and management questions.

Navigating between the CART and PRAT

Navigation between the CART in the PRAT is rather simple. There is a link to each in the menu on the upper left side of the screen. Using them allows you to jump between the two, and when selected will take you to the landing page for each sub-tool.

You can also use this methodology to switch questionnaires within CART and PRAT.

Navigating within the questionnaires

The following descriptions apply to both the CART and PRAT.

Once you have opened a questionnaire, you will see the various sections presented in a tabular format above the question. Nine of the ten sections correspond to one of the HSI domains. The tenth is a general/integration sections comprised of those questions that apply to more than one domain. Each tab functions as a drop down. When you hover over and click on the arrow in the selected tab, a drop-down menu will appear which contains a list of all the questions. If you click on a particular question, you will be taken directly to its page. For example, if you click question 1 of the General Tab under the AoA questionnaire (Figure 10), you will be taken to its answer page. This methodology can be used to jump between sections by simply selecting a question in a domain other than the one you are working on.

Using the Tool

The HSI tool is designed to support HSI analysts and program managers with the identification, documentation, and management of HSI-related risks, concerns, and considerations across all HSI domains as well as their relationships with the other domains in order to optimize total system performance while keeping total lifecycle costs down. Both sub-tools leverage a risk management approach to help guide users through execution of the HSI program, focusing on the Total Lifecycle.

Answering Questions

Each question prompts a yes or no answer. You have the option to skip the question by using the button in the lower right corner of the screen to advance to the next question. If you skip the question, it will automatically be given a "not applicable" answer until otherwise changed to "yes" or "no." You can also backtrack to the previous question using the button in the lower left corner of the screen.

Answering a Question "yes"

If you click the "Yes" button to answer the question as "yes," you will be presented with a text box labeled "Rationale" (see Figure 12). You can use this optional text box to provide details, justification, or other notes related to the question and why it was answered as it was. Answering a Question "No"

If you click the "No" button to answer the question as "No," you will be prompted to address questions relating to programmatic risk. The first items you will be presented with are the Risk Matrix and "Description of Risks" text box (Figure 13).


Figure 13. Risk Matrix and Description

Risk matrix

The Risk Matrix prompts you to rate the likelihood and consequence of occurrence of the risk based on subjective subject matter expertise. Likelihood is defined as the frequency at which the risk could be expected to occur, and is broken into five categories: unlikely, low likelihood, likely, very likely, and near constantly. Consequence is defined in terms of severity of the repercussions, and also has five categories each progressing in severity: minimal, minor, moderate, significant, and critical.

Each box on the matrix is assigned a color, either green, yellow, or red. These categorize each risk as either low and not addressed (green), medium requiring attention (yellow), significant requiring immediate action (red). Though presented in pastel, hovering over or selecting a box will fill it with a bold, solid hue to provide feedback that it has been selected.

Each box also has a numerical value assigned. Each likelihood and consequence category is assigned a number from one to five, where one is "unlikely" or "minimal" and five is "near constantly" or "critical." These numbers are cross-multiplied to combine the two factors into a single number, which is then assigned to that particular box. For example, a likely and significant risk is scored as a 12, while constant and critical risk is a 25. This facilitates prioritization of the highest risk items so they can be addressed accordingly.

Description of Risks

Use this box to identify and describe all risks (see Figure 14) associated with answering the question "No." That is, document each if-then risk statement. The current version of the tool does not allow you to rate each of these risks individually (i.e. with separate risk matrices and descriptions for identified risk). Therefore, it is necessary when scoring the risk on the matrix that all risks roll up to the highest risk. When a risk has been addressed, it will be necessary to go back to the question and manually update the Description box.


Figure 14. Description of Risks Text Box

Risk planning

Once you have documented the risks, you will see the "Risk Planning" dropdown. This options the risk handling questionnaire. The first question prompts you determine the overall mitigation strategy - you can either accept, transfer, avoid, or control the risks.

Accept: risk is tolerable, no actions required

Transfer: control of risk handling is given to a third party, such as the contractor

Avoid: prevent exposure to the risk in the first place

Control: risk is handled through active mitigation actions designed to specifically overcome the consequence, likelihood, or both
