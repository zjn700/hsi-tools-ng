GETTING STARTED

Welcome to the Human Systems Integration Risk Management Tool (HSI-RMT). This software suite consists of two sub-tools designed to guide Human Systems Integration (HSI) Analysts/Practitioners, Program Managers (PMs), and Chief Engineers, etc. through the process of accomplishing HSI best practices throughout system development. The first, the HSI Capabilities and Requirements Tool (HSI-CART) is designed to assist MAJCOM HSI Cells with the appropriate developmental planning and pre-Joint Capabilities Integration Development System (JCIDS) activities, such as Concept Development and Analysis of Alternatives. The second, the Program Risk Assessment Tool is designed to assist the system program office (SPO) with integrating HSI throughout the JCIDS process and acquisition life cycle, from Material Solution Analysis to Disposal planning. In this first section, we will introduce HSI and cover the basics of creating an account, logging in to your account, and basic navigation through the tool.

1.1 Introduction to Human Systems Integration

AFI 63-1201, Life Cycle Systems Engineering defines Human Systems Integration as “a disciplined, unified, and interactive systems engineering approach to integrate human considerations into the system development, design, and life cycle management to improve total system performance and reduce total costs of ownership.” Those human considerations span nine U.S. Air Force recognized HSI domains:

  1. Human Factors Engineering – Design characteristics for human capabilities and limitations
  2. Manpower – Assessment of the human capital requirements (manning mix) necessary to operate, maintain, and support the system.
  3. Personnel – Assessment of the attributes (knowledge, skills, and abilities) required to operate, maintain, and support the system.
  4. Training – design and development of instruction, resources, and support required to impart system end users with the required knowledge, skills, and abilities necessary to operate, maintain, and support the system.
  5. Environment – Assessment of those operational environmental factors and how they impact system design, as well as how the design impacts the operational environment.
  6. Safety – Assessment of system design characteristics and associated procedures that serve to minimize mishap potential.
  7. Occupational Health – Assessment of system design features that serve to minimize the risks for injury, illness, or disability, and enhance job performance.
  8. Survivability – Consideration of system design features which reduce the likelihood for death or serious injury, to include consideration for hostile environments, fratricide, and emergency egress.
  9. Habitability – Integrated considerations of the system and associated facilities’ impact social interactions, morale, quality of life, safety and comfort, etc. to sustain maximum performance.

Human Systems Integration is a unique discipline in that is it both a technical and managerial philosophy. Per Department of Defense Instruction 5000.02, program managers are required to plan for and implement HSI through the product lifecycle. The earlier HSI is introduced, the more likely it is to reap the long-term benefits of risk mitigation, reduced redesign and rework, mishap reduction, etc. and achieve the desired life cycle cost savings. If properly included in system development, Analysts and Practitioners integrate and examine the risks and tradeoffs amongst the nine domains balance against life cycle cost considerations to provide consultation and decision making support to the Program Manager, ensuring that human considerations are adequately accounted for. In this manner, developers are able to drive total system performance and minimize total life cycle cost.

HSI is not just another name for Human Factors or just “good Systems Engineering”. Rather, Human Factors is just one of the disciplines that Analysts integrates – HSI includes so much more. In fact, a primary role of the Analyst/Practitioner is to integrate each of their own domains as well as those from other functional leads to advise their Program Managers and Systems Engineers on the integrated tradeoffs.

1.3 Create an Account

The "Create an account" link on the sign-in page will take you to the sign-up form (shown below). Enter the requested information, confirm your password, then click the submit button. Note that the submit button will not work until you have entered all requested information. If you successfully created an account, you will be returned to the sign in form (Figure 2). Enter your credentials there to sign-in.

Figure 3. Create an Account Page

Note: Once signed in you will have the option of editing your contact information (name and email). You will also be able to enter additional contact information (office title/symbol and phone numbers).

1.4 Sign-in to the HSI-RMT

If you have not yet created an account, please refer to section 1.3 Creating an Account. If you have already created one, you must sign-in in order to create and access projects. Enter your email address and password in the appropriate boxes and then click the “Submit” button to log in.

Figure 3. HSI-RMT - Sign-In Page

1.5 Create a Project

Once you have successfully logged in, you will be taken to the HSI-RMT Project Landing Page. This page allows you to create, select, and modify projects. If you have not yet created a project, you will see the message shown below. Click the green plus button on the lower right side of the screen to create your first project.
